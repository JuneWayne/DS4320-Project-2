# Can You Tell the Difference? How Machine Learning Is Being Reprogrammed to Spot AI-Generated Text

## Hook

Everyday, millions of people read AI-generated text and are unknowingly consuming the knowledge from those within. From social media posts to news articles to product reviews, large language models are gradually polluting the human knowledge base with resampled texts generated from a machine that has no real creativity or insightful perspective. Our future knowledge base is at risk, and it is time to start using the machine against itself, to flag and classify what is real and what is not.

## Problem Statement

In the status quo, commercial detectors like GPTZero and traditional plagiarism detectors like Turnitin are too unreliable and can be easily fooled by any state of the art LLMs in the market. The rapid development of GPT 5.X, Claude, and Gemini has made AI generated texts increasingly difficult to distinguish from human writing. Existing platforms exhibit high false positive rates and high negative rates in their predictions. Legitimate human writings are flagged as AI generated content, which has led to real consequences and confusions among students in academic settings, while real AI generated texts are left uncaught and still out in the wild being exploited by online spam bots to create misinformation. There is a desperate need for a new, more reliable AI text detector that is up to date with SOTA models to ensure that we are still able to compete and catch disingenuous content before it reaches the majority of its targeted audience online.

## Solution Description

In this project, I built an end-to-end ML system that was trained on a diverse set of data spanning multiple LLMs, content domain subjects, and generation strategies that encompasses all types of text that can be generated by existing state of the art LLMs. In doing so, I created a robust machine learning classifier that detects the intricate statistical patterns of the tokens generated, such as word count, vocabulary diversity, average sentence length, and punctuation density, to determine whether a text looks like those generated by an AI. The model combines these handcrafted linguistic features with TF-IDF text representations to capture both broad stylistic patterns and vocabulary-level signals. Given that this model was trained on a diverse range of text types, the end product is designed to be reusable, robust, and generalizable against all types of modern AI generated texts. The model produces a probability score for its inputs and a label indicating whether the text is highly likely to be AI generated or not.

The chart above shows the words and phrases that most strongly push the classifier toward predicting AI or human authorship. The red bars on the right are words and bigrams that the model associates with AI generated text, things like "ingredients", "instructions", "overall", and "is based", which reflect the formulaic and structured language that AI models tend to produce across recipe, abstract, and news domains. The blue bars on the left are words the model associates with human writing, dominated by basic function words like "is", "and", "of", and "the", as well as some HTML artifacts like "br" that came from Reddit and review text in the human corpus. The key takeaway here is that AI writing tends to be more content-heavy and structured, while human writing relies more on basic connective language and informal patterns that AI models tend to underuse.
